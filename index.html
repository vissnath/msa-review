01 / 12 Who covers legal costs? Watch out
What the contract says
If anyone ever sues or makes a claim connected to your use of this service, you have to pay all of the vendor's legal bills — no matter who is actually at fault.
What that means for you
Imagine the vendor's software causes a data leak, and your customers sue. Under this clause, you would be paying the lawyers — even though the problem started with the vendor. There is no limit on how much this could cost.
Our advice
Do not sign this as written.
We need to push back and make this a two-way street — both sides responsible only for problems they actually caused. We'll also ask for a cap on costs so there's no unlimited exposure.
02 / 12 How much can you claim if things go wrong? Let's negotiate
What the contract says
If the vendor causes you a loss, the most you can ever recover from them is one month of your subscription fees.
What that means for you
If a serious incident costs your business €50,000 but you only pay €2,000 a month, you'd only recover €2,000. The rest is your problem.
Our advice
We can get this improved.
The industry standard is 12 months of fees, not 1. We'll negotiate for at least 6 months, and push for data breaches to have a higher separate limit.
03 / 12 How long does the contract last? Looks fine
What the contract says
The contract runs for one year and auto-renews unless either side gives 60 days' notice before the end date.
What that means for you
You have 60 days before each anniversary to decide if you want to leave. Miss that window and you're committed for another year. Set a calendar reminder now — 90 days before the renewal date.
Our advice
Fine to accept.
A 60-day notice window and annual renewal are completely standard. Just make sure you have a reminder in your calendar well in advance.
04 / 12 Can they change the price? Let's negotiate
What the contract says
The vendor can increase fees at any renewal with only 30 days' notice, with no cap on how much prices can go up.
What that means for you
They could double the price before your next renewal and you'd only find out 30 days before. By then it may be too late to find an alternative.
Our advice
We should cap the annual increase.
We'll ask for rises to be limited — either linked to inflation or a fixed 5% max per year — and 90 days' notice so you have time to act.
05 / 12 Who owns your data? Watch out
What the contract says
The vendor is granted a broad licence to use, copy, and analyse any data you put into the platform — including for improving their own products.
What that means for you
Your customer data, financial records, or business information could legally be used to train the vendor's AI or benefit their other customers. This is a serious confidentiality and GDPR concern.
Our advice
This needs to be fixed before you sign.
Your data should be used only to deliver the service to you — nothing else. We'll rewrite this clause to say exactly that.
06 / 12 Keeping information confidential Looks fine
What the contract says
Both parties agree not to share each other's confidential business information, for three years after the contract ends.
What that means for you
Anything sensitive you share with the vendor stays private — and vice versa — for three years even after you stop using the service.
Our advice
Balanced and reasonable.
Three years is standard. The protection applies equally to both sides. No changes needed.
07 / 12 Can they stop the service suddenly? Let's negotiate
What the contract says
The vendor can suspend your access immediately if they believe you have breached any term — with no warning and no chance to fix the issue first.
What that means for you
If there's ever a dispute — even a misunderstanding about an invoice — they could switch off your access the same day. If this system is important to your daily operations, that could be very disruptive.
Our advice
We should add a warning period.
We'll ask for a 30-day notice and cure period — they must tell you about the issue and give you time to fix it before cutting access. Immediate termination should only apply to fraud or non-payment.
08 / 12 Which country's laws apply? Looks fine
What the contract says
Any disputes will be governed by Dutch law and settled in the courts of Amsterdam.
What that means for you
If you ever end up in a dispute, it's handled under Dutch law in Amsterdam. Since you're based in the Netherlands, this is actually in your favour.
Our advice
This works well for you.
Dutch jurisdiction is a genuinely good outcome. No changes needed.
09 / 12 Are they promising the service will work? Let's negotiate
What the contract says
The vendor promises 99% uptime per year, but their only remedy for falling short is a small credit off your next invoice.
What that means for you
99% still allows over 87 hours of downtime per year. And if the system goes down at a critical moment, you get a discount voucher — not compensation for actual losses.
Our advice
We can improve this.
We'll push for 99.9% uptime (around 9 hours per year maximum) and the right to exit without penalty if they miss this target repeatedly.
10 / 12 Can they change the product without telling you? Watch out
What the contract says
The vendor can change, discontinue, or remove any features at any time without notice or compensation.
What that means for you
If your team relies on a specific feature — say, an integration with your accounting software — the vendor could remove it overnight. You'd be locked into a contract for a product that no longer does what you paid for.
Our advice
This clause needs to be rewritten.
We need 90 days' advance notice before any material feature is removed, and the right to exit without penalty if a change significantly affects how you use the product.
11 / 12 Can they pass the contract to someone else? Let's negotiate
What the contract says
The vendor can transfer this contract to any other company without your permission. You cannot transfer it without theirs.
What that means for you
The vendor could be bought by a competitor and your contract transfers automatically. You'd have no say. Meanwhile, you can't make changes on your side without asking them first.
Our advice
We'll ask for a mutual consent requirement.
Both sides should need the other's agreement to transfer. At minimum, we want the right to exit — penalty-free — if the vendor is acquired by a company you're not comfortable with.
12 / 12 What happens to your data when you leave? Looks fine
What the contract says
When the contract ends, the vendor will export all your data within 30 days and permanently delete it from their systems within 60 days.
What that means for you
When you leave, you get your data back and the vendor can't keep it on their servers. This makes switching providers much easier.
Our advice
Good — keep it exactly as is.
Clear timelines and a deletion commitment are exactly what you want. We'll just clarify that the data is exported in a standard readable format.
Negotiation guide

You've never done this before. Here's exactly how it works.

Contract negotiation follows a very predictable pattern. Once you know the steps and what to say, it's much less intimidating than it sounds.

The four steps
Step 1
Send your asks in writing
Email them your points. No call needed yet — writing gives them time to consult internally.
Step 2
Wait for their response
They'll accept some, push back on others. This is completely normal. Don't panic.
Step 3
One short call to close
A 30-minute call to work through any remaining points. Keep it focused.
Step 4
Confirm everything in writing
Whatever was agreed on the call gets confirmed by email before signing.
The right mindset
Think this

"This is a normal business conversation. Asking for changes is expected — vendors deal with this every day."

Not this

"If I push back they'll walk away or think I'm difficult." Vendors want your business. They won't walk over reasonable asks.

Think this

"I don't need to win everything. I need to fix the 3 serious issues and improve the rest where I can."

Not this

"I need to challenge every single clause." Picking your battles signals confidence, not weakness.

Detailed guidance
Clause 1 — Who covers legal costs? Must fix
Your targets
Ideal: mutual + capped at 12 months fees Fallback: capped at contract value Walk if: still one-sided and uncapped
What to say
"We reviewed clause 1 on indemnification. As written, we carry unlimited legal liability for any claim connected to use of the platform — including claims that may originate on your side. That's not something we can accept.

We'd like to make this mutual: each party responsible for claims arising from their own actions. We'd also want to cap total exposure at 12 months of fees paid. This is standard market practice and we hope it's a straightforward change."
Clause 2 — How much can you claim? Negotiate
Your targets
Ideal: 12 months + data breach outside cap Fallback: 6 months fees Walk if: stays at 1 month
What to say
"On the liability cap — one month of fees is well below what we see in comparable agreements. A service failure could cost us multiples of that in lost business or compliance exposure.

We'd ask for the cap to be set at 12 months of total fees. We'd also like data breaches to sit outside the cap entirely, given our GDPR obligations. We're open to discussing the number, but one month simply isn't workable."
Clause 4 — Can they change the price? Negotiate
Your targets
Ideal: CPI-linked cap + 90 days notice Fallback: max 5% per year + 60 days Walk if: completely uncapped increases
What to say
"Clause 4 gives you the right to increase pricing without any limit and with only 30 days' notice. That makes proper budgeting very difficult on our end.

We'd like annual increases capped — either at CPI or a fixed maximum of 5% per year — and at least 90 days' notice so we can plan or review our options. We understand prices need to move. We just need it to be predictable."
Clause 5 — Who owns your data? Must fix
Your targets
Ideal: data used only to deliver the service Fallback: anonymised analytics only Walk if: broad licence to use customer data remains
What to say
"Clause 5 grants you a broad licence to use and analyse our data for product improvement. We can't agree to that.

We own our data. Any licence should be strictly limited to delivering the contracted service to us — nothing more. We'd also want an explicit statement that our data will not be used to train models or benefit any third party. We also have GDPR obligations here that make this clause a compliance risk we simply cannot carry."
Clause 7 — Can they stop the service suddenly? Negotiate
Your targets
Ideal: 30-day notice + cure period Fallback: immediate only for fraud/non-payment Walk if: totally unrestricted right to suspend
What to say
"Clause 7 lets you suspend our access immediately for any perceived breach — no notice, no chance to respond. If this platform is part of our operations, an unexpected suspension would be genuinely disruptive.

We'd like a cure period: if you believe we're in breach, you notify us and we have 30 days to resolve it before suspension can take effect. Immediate action should only apply to fraud or non-payment."
Clause 10 — Can they change the product? Must fix
Your targets
Ideal: 90-day notice + exit right Fallback: 60 days + price reduction right Walk if: no notice and no exit right at all
What to say
"Clause 10 allows you to remove any feature at any time with no notice. We can't rely on or budget for a service under those terms.

We'd ask for a minimum of 90 days' written notice before any material feature change or removal. We'd also want the right to exit without penalty if a change materially affects our ability to use the product as we're purchasing it today. We're not trying to freeze your product — we just need enough notice to adapt."
"This is our standard contract — we don't make changes."
Almost never entirely true. Respond calmly:
"We understand you have a standard form and we're not trying to redraft it entirely. We have three specific clauses that create real legal and regulatory exposure for us. We'd like to work through just those."
"No other customer has ever asked for this."
Probably not true — and even if it is, it's irrelevant. Reply:
"That may be, but our legal and compliance requirements are specific to us. These changes are standard in the market and we'd like to find a way to make them work for both sides."
"If we change this for you, we'd have to change it for everyone."
Not your problem — but be diplomatic:
"We understand the operational challenge. That said, the clause as written creates liability we genuinely can't accept. Perhaps we can find wording that works within your framework."
"Legal has already approved this and can't reopen it."
Ask to escalate directly:
"We'd be happy to speak directly with your legal team. Our concerns are specific and I think they'd be straightforward to resolve in a short conversation."
"We'll come halfway — take it or leave it."
Assess what they're actually offering. For "must fix" clauses, halfway is usually not enough. For "negotiate" clauses it may be fine:
"We appreciate the movement. Let us look at the specific language and come back to you — we want to get this across the line."
1
Always negotiate in writing first. Email gives you a record, lets them consult internally, and removes the pressure of a live call. Save the call for when you're nearly there.
2
Lead with the "why", not the demand. "We carry GDPR obligations" lands better than "change clause 5." People respond to reasons.
3
Give them easy wins. Explicitly accept the clauses that are fine. It signals you're being fair and makes them more likely to move on the ones that matter.
4
Never reveal your walk-away point. Know it internally — don't say "we'll sign if you just fix the data clause." Let them come to you.
5
Silence is fine. After you send your asks, wait. Don't follow up within a day softening your position. Give them time.
6
Confirm everything in writing. End every call with: "I'll send a summary of what we agreed." Then do it the same day. Nothing is agreed until it's in writing.
