Common questions
What types of contracts does Joan review? ⌄
Joan works best with Master Services Agreements (MSAs), NDAs, SaaS subscription agreements, freelance contracts, and general service agreements — basically any contract written in English where someone is asking you to sign something before you fully understand it.
Is Joan a lawyer? ⌄
No — Joan is an AI tool, not a lawyer, and nothing Joan says is legal advice. Joan helps you understand what's in your contract and gives you language to start a negotiation. For high-stakes or complex contracts, we always recommend working with a qualified lawyer.
How does Joan analyse my contract? ⌄
You upload a PDF or Word file. Joan extracts the text, identifies individual clauses, and analyses each one for common risk patterns — things like uncapped liability, one-sided indemnification, broad data licences, and unreasonable termination rights. Each clause gets a plain-English explanation, a risk flag, and where relevant, a suggested rewrite.
Is my contract kept private? ⌄
Your contract is processed securely and is never stored permanently or used to train AI models. We take confidentiality seriously — what you upload stays between you and Joan.
What does Joan give me at the end? ⌄
For each clause Joan gives you: a plain-English explanation of what it says, a risk rating (watch out / let's negotiate / looks fine), a suggested rewrite where changes are needed, and a ready-to-send response you can copy directly into an email. At the end you get a complete negotiation email drafted and ready to go.
I've never negotiated a contract before. Can Joan help? ⌄
That's exactly who Joan is built for. The negotiation guide walks you through the process step by step — what to say, when to say it, how to handle pushback, and what the golden rules are. You don't need any legal background to use Joan effectively.

01 / 12 Who covers legal costs? Watch out
What the contract says
If anyone ever sues or makes a claim connected to your use of this service, you have to pay all of the vendor's legal bills — no matter who is actually at fault.
What that means for you
Imagine the vendor's software causes a data leak and your customers sue. Under this clause, you would be paying the lawyers — even though the problem started with the vendor. There is no limit on how much this could cost.
Our advice
Do not sign this as written.
We need to push back and make this a two-way street — both sides responsible only for problems they actually caused. We will also ask for a cap on costs.
Suggested rewritten clause
Redline suggestion
"Customer shall indemnify, defend, and hold harmless Vendor and its affiliates from and against any and all claims arising out of or related to Customer's use of the Services. Each party shall indemnify, defend, and hold harmless the other party from claims arising out of that party's own gross negligence or wilful misconduct, subject to the liability cap in Section 9.1. Vendor shall retain sole responsibility for indemnifying Customer against third-party IP infringement claims related to the Services."
Suggested response to the other party
"We reviewed clause 1 on indemnification. As written, we carry unlimited legal liability for any claim connected to use of the platform — including claims that may originate on your side. That's not something we can accept. We'd like to make this mutual: each party responsible for claims arising from their own actions or negligence. We'd also want to cap total exposure at 12 months of fees paid. This is standard market practice and we hope it's a straightforward change."

02 / 12 How much can you claim if things go wrong? Let's negotiate
What the contract says
If the vendor causes you a loss, the most you can ever recover is one month of your subscription fees.
What that means for you
If a serious incident costs your business €50,000 but you only pay €2,000 a month, you'd only recover €2,000. The rest is your problem.
Our advice
We can get this improved.
The industry standard is 12 months of fees, not 1. We'll negotiate for at least 6 months, and push for data breaches to have a higher separate limit.
Suggested rewritten clause
Redline suggestion
"In no event shall Vendor's aggregate liability exceed the fees paid by Customer in the one (1) month twelve (12) months preceding the claim. Notwithstanding the foregoing, claims arising from a data breach or security incident shall not be subject to this cap and shall be governed by applicable data protection law."
Suggested response to the other party
"On the liability cap — one month of fees is well below what we see in comparable agreements. A service failure could cost us multiples of that in lost business or compliance exposure. We'd ask for the cap to be set at 12 months of total fees paid. We'd also like data breaches to sit outside the cap entirely, given our GDPR obligations. We're open to discussing the right number, but one month simply isn't workable."

04 / 12 Can they change the price? Let's negotiate
What the contract says
The vendor can increase fees at any renewal with only 30 days' notice, with no cap on how much prices can go up.
What that means for you
They could double the price before your next renewal and you'd only find out 30 days before. By then it may be too late to find an alternative.
Our advice
We should cap the annual increase.
We'll ask for rises to be limited — either linked to inflation or a fixed 5% max per year — and 90 days' notice so you have time to act.
Suggested rewritten clause
Redline suggestion
"Vendor may adjust fees at renewal by providing thirty (30) ninety (90) days' written notice. Any such increase shall not exceed the lesser of five percent (5%) per annum or the applicable CPI inflation rate for the preceding 12 months."
Suggested response to the other party
"Clause 4 gives you the right to increase pricing without any limit and with only 30 days' notice. That makes proper budgeting very difficult on our end. We'd like annual increases capped — either at CPI or a fixed maximum of 5% per year — and at least 90 days' notice so we can plan or review our options."

05 / 12 Who owns your data? Watch out
What the contract says
The vendor is granted a broad licence to use, copy, and analyse any data you put into the platform — including for improving their own products.
What that means for you
Your customer data, financial records, or business information could legally be used to train the vendor's AI or benefit their other customers. This is a serious confidentiality and GDPR concern.
Our advice
This needs to be fixed before you sign.
Your data should be used only to deliver the service to you — nothing else. We'll rewrite this clause to say exactly that.
Suggested rewritten clause
Redline suggestion
"Customer grants Vendor a limited, non-exclusive licence to process Customer Data solely and for any purpose including product improvement, benchmarking and service development to the extent necessary to provide the Services to Customer. Vendor shall not use Customer Data for any other purpose, including training machine learning models, benchmarking, or any benefit to third parties. Customer retains all ownership of Customer Data at all times."
Suggested response to the other party
"Clause 5 grants you a broad licence to use and analyse our data for product improvement. We can't agree to that. We own our data. Any licence should be strictly limited to delivering the contracted service to us — nothing more. We'd also want an explicit statement that our data will not be used to train models or benefit any third party. We also have GDPR obligations here that make this clause a compliance risk we simply cannot carry."

What that means for you

10 / 12 Can they change the product without telling you? Watch out
What the contract says
The vendor can change, discontinue, or remove any features at any time without notice or compensation.
What that means for you
If your team relies on a specific feature, the vendor could remove it overnight. You'd be locked into a contract for a product that no longer does what you paid for.
Our advice
This clause needs to be rewritten.
We need 90 days' advance notice before any material feature is removed, and the right to exit without penalty if a change significantly affects how you use the product.
Suggested rewritten clause
Redline suggestion
"Vendor may update or modify the Services at any time without notice or compensation to Customer provided that Vendor gives Customer at least ninety (90) days' written notice prior to any material change, removal, or discontinuation of a feature. Suggested response to the other party
"Clause 10 allows you to remove any feature at any time with no notice. We can't rely on or budget for a service under those terms. We'd ask for a minimum of 90 days' written notice before any material feature change or removal, and the right to exit without penalty if a change materially affects our ability to use the product as we're purchasing it today."

What the contract says

What that means for you
The vendor could be bought by a competitor and your contract transfers automatically. You'd have no say in the matter.
Our advice
We'll ask for a mutual consent requirement.

Suggested rewritten clause

"Neither party may assign or transfer this Agreement without the prior written consent of the other party, provided that Vendor may assign this Agreement without consent in connection with a merger, acquisition or sale of all or substantially all of its assets. except that in the event of a change of control of Vendor, Customer shall have the right to terminate this Agreement without penalty within 60 days of receiving written notice of such change."
Suggested response to the other party
"Clause 11 allows you to transfer this agreement to any third party — including in a merger or acquisition — without our consent. At minimum, we'd like the right to exit the contract without penalty if you are acquired by a direct competitor of ours."

Negotiation guide

You've never done this before. Here's exactly how it works.

Contract negotiation follows a very predictable pattern. Once you know the steps and what to say, it's much less intimidating than it sounds.

The four steps
Step 1
Send your asks in writing
Email them your points. No call needed yet — writing gives them time to consult internally.
Step 2
Wait for their response
They'll accept some, push back on others. This is completely normal. Don't panic.
Step 3
One short call to close
A 30-minute call to work through any remaining points. Keep it focused.
Step 4
Confirm everything in writing
Whatever was agreed on the call gets confirmed by email before signing.
The right mindset
Think this

"This is a normal business conversation. Asking for changes is expected — vendors deal with this every day."

Not this

"If I push back they'll walk away or think I'm difficult." Vendors want your business. They won't walk over reasonable asks.

Think this

"I don't need to win everything. I need to fix the 3 serious issues and improve the rest where I can."

Not this

"I need to challenge every single clause." Picking your battles signals confidence, not weakness.

Detailed guidance
"This is our standard contract — we don't make changes."
Almost never entirely true. Respond calmly:
"We understand you have a standard form and we're not trying to redraft it entirely. We have three specific clauses that create real legal and regulatory exposure for us. We'd like to work through just those."
"No other customer has ever asked for this."
Probably not true — and even if it is, it's irrelevant. Reply:
"That may be, but our legal and compliance requirements are specific to us. These changes are standard in the market and we'd like to find a way to make them work for both sides."
"If we change this for you, we'd have to change it for everyone."
Not your problem — but be diplomatic:
"We understand the operational challenge. That said, the clause as written creates liability we genuinely can't accept. Perhaps we can find wording that works within your framework."
"Legal has already approved this and can't reopen it."
Ask to escalate directly:
"We'd be happy to speak directly with your legal team. Our concerns are specific and I think they'd be straightforward to resolve in a short conversation."
"We'll come halfway — take it or leave it."
Assess what they're actually offering. For "must fix" clauses, halfway is usually not enough:
"We appreciate the movement. Let us look at the specific language and come back to you — we want to get this across the line."
1
Always negotiate in writing first. Email gives you a record, lets them consult internally, and removes the pressure of a live call.
2
Lead with the "why", not the demand. "We carry GDPR obligations" lands better than "change clause 5."
3
Give them easy wins. Explicitly accept the clauses that are fine. It signals you're being fair and makes them more likely to move on the ones that matter.
4
Never reveal your walk-away point. Know it internally — don't say "we'll sign if you just fix the data clause."
5
Silence is fine. After you send your asks, wait. Don't follow up within a day softening your position.
6
Confirm everything in writing. End every call with: "I'll send a summary of what we agreed." Then do it the same day.
